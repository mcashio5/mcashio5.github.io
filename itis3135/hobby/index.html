What: The Joy of Fishing

My favorite hobby is fishing—mostly freshwater for bass and catfish, and surf fishing at the beach for flounder, mackerel, and (tiny) baby sharks. I love the patience, the patterns, and the surprise when the rod bends.

Why freshwater?

Accessible, peaceful, and a great way to learn reading structure, seasonal patterns, and lure selection.

Why surf?

The beach changes daily—currents, troughs, and tides make every session a puzzle with moving pieces.

Who: Bass, Cats & Beach Bites

I’m a weekend angler who splits time between local lakes and the Carolina coast. When I’m not testing spinnerbaits for bass or cut bait for catfish, I’m scouting sandbars looking for flounder ambush points and mackerel runs.

When: Seasons & Bite Windows

Timing matters. For freshwater bass, spring and fall are peak; catfish like warm evenings. Surf fishing keys on tides and moving water—dawn and dusk around a good tide swing are money.

Freshwater: Pre-spawn (spring) & fall shad runs.
Surf: 1–2 hours around incoming/outgoing tide.
Night: Catfish & sharks (follow local regs).

Where: Lakes, Piers & Troughs

On lakes, I target points, grass lines, and creek mouths. On the beach, I read the surf: look for darker “cuts,” sandbar gaps, and rips that funnel bait (and predators).

  • Lakes: points adjacent to deep water, windblown banks, docks with shade.
  • Surf: trough edges, near inlets, around bait schools.
  • Piers: current breaks, shade lines, live bait rigs for flounder.

How: Gear, Rigs & Simple Plan

Keep it simple: one medium spinning combo for bass, one heavier setup for catfish, and a corrosion-safe surf rod for the beach. Match your rig to the target and conditions.

Why: Patience, Patterns, Peace

Fishing teaches patience and pattern recognition—weather, water, seasons, forage. It’s also a way to reset mentally: slow down, watch, learn, and enjoy being outside.

Respect the water: follow local regulations, practice safe handling, and pack out all trash.
